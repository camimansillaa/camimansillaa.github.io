The Story Behind Hikaru Dorodango

Hikaru Dorodango (光る泥だんご) literally means “shining mud dumpling.” It’s a Japanese craft that turns ordinary soil into a glossy, mirror-smooth sphere through patient layering and polishing.

Children in Japan have shaped mud balls for generations, but in the late 1990s Professor Fumio Kanda of Kyoto University helped transform it into an art form. His research on surface tension and moisture balance inspired modern hobbyists to experiment with new techniques.

Rooted in Simplicity

The dorodango process embodies wabi-sabi — the appreciation of imperfection and transience. Each ball records the maker’s patience, rhythm, and sense of balance.

More Than Mud

Every layer of soil, dust, and polish represents connection to nature. By transforming dirt into shine, artists reveal hidden beauty in ordinary materials.

A Global Revival

Today dorodango artists around the world share techniques online, host exhibitions, and explore color blending using pigments, sand, and clay variations.

“In shaping earth with patience, we shape ourselves.” Traditional proverb
